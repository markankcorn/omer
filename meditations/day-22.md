We move this week to an examination of Netzach, usually translated as endurance or victory, though the word points to ideas like persistence, drive, ambition, and what the Hasidic masters called confidence. It's the part of you that doesn't give up. And as always, we begin the week with love.

The surface view of endurance is that it runs on willpower alone, with gritted teeth, a clenched jaw, "I will get through this." It works for a while, but like every coping mechanism it doesn't come for free. It hardens you. You end up resenting the very thing you outlasted, which is a tell that you were never really in Netzach to begin with. That's a joyless Gevurah wearing Netzach's coat.

The love we're pointing at today is not the love of Hesed week. That love was overflowing, generous, uncomplicated, the love before the weather hits. The love inside endurance is a different animal: it's love *after* weather. A parent up at 3 a.m. with a sick child isn't running on willpower. A relationship that makes it through the flat years didn't get there by gritted teeth. A craft pursued for decades has it. It's often quiet, sometimes invisible even to the person carrying it.

So ask yourself today: where in your life are you enduring without love? If you're gritting your teeth against a job, a relationship, a project, a goal — is there love inside all that fatigue that wants to be found again? Or is it just a competitive urge not to lose, not to quit? Being honest with yourself is a crucial part of the work we're doing. 

Netzach doesn't mean holding on to everything forever. It means that what you're holding on to is alive and filled with love surging through it and making the work worthwhile. Fight for love, endure for love; don't fight just to win. Those trophies aren't worth it.
